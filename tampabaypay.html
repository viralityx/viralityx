Market & Portfolio Context

Your book's value is a multiple of residual

Buyers value a processing portfolio at roughly 22 to 30x monthly residual. A clean book earns the top of that range, a leaky one earns the bottom. Everything you do to the book either builds or erodes that number.

Attrition sets the multiple

Books under 10% annual attrition earn premium multiples. Books that shed 25%+ in a year get paid at the bottom of the range. Every point of churn you cut is multiplied into enterprise value.

You already sell more than payments

Websites, SEO, GoHighLevel, POS. Your 250+ merchants trust you as a technology partner, not just a processor. That trusted book is a distribution channel almost no other ISO has.

Your moat is service, and service is manual

A 10-person team promising a direct line to 250+ merchants is your edge. It does not scale linearly. Without leverage, growth makes the promise harder to keep.

Competitive Landscape

Local ISOs (PayFrog, Nock Pay, EMS)

PayFrog has run Tampa since 2010 on the same transparent, no-contract message you use. Nock Pay targets restaurants, e-commerce, and healthcare. They compete on your exact pitch, so rate alone is a race to the bottom. Stickiness and service are how you win, not price.

The PayFac threat (Square, Stripe, Toast)

This is the real attrition risk. They win on instant signup and software, then freeze funds and hide behind an 800 number. Your true merchant account, interchange-plus pricing, and local support are the counter, and for any merchant above $15 to 20k/mo in volume, you are the cheaper sustainable option.

Where you win

Local, human, transparent, no contracts, next-day funding, and a 5.0 reputation. On a level field you beat both the local ISOs and the PayFacs. The gap is that nothing systematizes that advantage or defends it after the sale.

Where you're exposed

Your differentiators live in people's heads and inboxes. A competitor undercutting on rate, or a Square rep promising simplicity, can pull a merchant you have no early warning on. That is the exact hole a retention engine closes.

Regulatory Signal — Dual Pricing

You lead with dual pricing, and the market is moving your way. Over 60% of SMBs now run surcharge or dual pricing, and dual pricing is legal in all 50 states. But the rules are shifting. Virginia and Minnesota passed "all-in pricing" laws in 2025 that push merchants off surcharging and toward dual pricing, and non-compliance fines run $50K to $1M. Every merchant you put on dual pricing needs correct signage, 30-day card-brand notice, and ongoing compliance. That is a manual burden today and an automation opportunity tomorrow, and being the processor who keeps merchants compliant is one more reason they never leave.

Bottlenecks

1. No proactive retention system on the residual book

The book is defended by great service, but service is reactive. There is no automated early-warning on merchants whose volume is dropping, whose statements signal they are shopping, or who have gone quiet. In this business, a churned merchant is not one lost sale, it is a lost recurring residual stream and a cut to portfolio value.

ESTIMATED MONTHLY LEAK: $9,000
Assumption: 250+ merchant book. Industry valuation data treats under 10%/yr attrition as a clean book and 25%+/yr as a leaky one; at a midpoint, roughly 3 to 4 merchants lost per month at an estimated ~$120/mo average residual. Modeled as eroding recurring residual plus the reacquisition cost to stay flat. Each lost residual dollar also cuts portfolio value at the 22 to 30x multiple. Estimate pending operator validation.

2. The statement / rate analysis is manual, and it is your sales engine

Your best closing tool is the line-by-line statement breakdown that shows a merchant exactly what they overpay. Today a human does that for every prospect. That caps how many merchants each rep can work, and slow turnaround loses deals to whoever answered first.

ESTIMATED MONTHLY LEAK: $8,000
Assumption: manual analysis limits throughput. Instant analysis could realistically add ~2 signings/mo at ~$120/mo residual with multi-year life, plus reclaimed rep time. Modeled conservatively at ~$8K/mo in delayed and lost opportunity.

3. Speed-to-lead on statement uploads and consult requests

A merchant who uploads a statement or books a consult is shopping, and they are usually talking to more than one processor. If that upload is not worked within minutes, the warmest moment is gone. There is no sign of instant acknowledgment or automated routing behind the rate-review form.

ESTIMATED MONTHLY LEAK: $6,000
Assumption: ~2 convertible merchants lost per quarter to slow response, valued at residual lifetime and spread monthly (~$6K/mo).

4. The hands-on support promise does not scale with the team

The brand is built on a direct line and no 800 number for 250+ merchants. As the book grows, support volume pulls account managers and execs off selling and retention. The promise that wins merchants becomes the thing that caps growth.

ESTIMATED MONTHLY LEAK: $4,000
Assumption: rising support load diverts ~$4K/mo of selling and retention capacity from a ~10-person team, plus the risk of the service promise slipping as volume grows.

5. The 250-merchant book is an unmonetized distribution channel

You already resell websites, SEO, and GoHighLevel. Your merchants trust you. That same book wants AI voice agents, review engines, CRM, and automation, and almost none of them have it. Right now that channel sits unworked, and it is the same offer that would make those merchants stickier.

ESTIMATED MONTHLY LEAK: $5,000 unrealized
Assumption: a 10% attach across 250 merchants at ~$200/mo margin to Tampa Bay Pay is ~$5K/mo of unrealized recurring revenue, ramping. It also directly lowers attrition on Bottleneck 1.
